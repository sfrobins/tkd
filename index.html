Cultural Heritage

The Hwarang Warriors

The Hwarang (화랑, "Flowering Knights") were an elite group of young warriors in the Silla Kingdom (57 BC–935 AD). Trained in martial arts, poetry, music, and moral philosophy, they embodied the ideal of the Korean warrior-scholar.

Their code of ethics — loyalty, filial piety, trust, courage, and justice — directly influenced the tenets of modern Tae Kwon Do. The Hwa-Rang form (2nd Gup) is named in their honour.

"The sword is not for killing but for the perfection of character."

Full Story

The Birth of ITF

On April 11, 1955, Korean martial arts were unified under the name Tae Kwon Do, meaning "the way of the foot and fist." General Choi Hong Hi, a Korean Army general and martial arts master, had been the driving force behind this unification.

More than a decade later, on March 22, 1966, General Choi founded the International Taekwon-Do Federation (ITF) in Seoul, Korea. His goal was to spread the art globally while preserving its authentic Korean roots and philosophical depth.

The ITF style is distinguished by its emphasis on the sine wave motion — a unique way of generating power by flowing up and down in a natural, wave-like rhythm that maximizes force at the moment of impact.

Today, millions of practitioners worldwide train in the ITF style, honouring General Choi's legacy of discipline, cultural pride, and the indomitable human spirit. Grand Master Scott Craig carries this lineage forward with every lesson.

Form Overview

Dan-Gun Tul consists of 21 movements. It is the second form in the ITF curriculum and introduces the walking stance and high block. The pattern diagram forms the shape of the Korean letter 工 (I-shape), symbolising the meeting of Heaven and Earth.

High blocks (Nopunde Makgi) form the primary defensive technique

Walking stance (Gunnun Sogi) transitions must be sharp and precise

Maintain correct weight distribution throughout each stance

Cultural & Historical Context

Dan-Gun Wanggeom (단군왕검) is the legendary founder of Gojoseon, the first Korean kingdom. According to legend, he was the son of Hwan-ung (a son of the Heavenly King) and a woman transformed from a bear.

Dan-Gun founded Gojoseon in 2333 BC at Asadal, establishing a civilisation built on virtue, wisdom, and harmony with nature. His rule is said to have lasted over a thousand years, and his legacy defines the Korean national identity to this day.
